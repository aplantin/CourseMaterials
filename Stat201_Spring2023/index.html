1 Introduction

1.1 Why Are We Here?

Statistics is the art and science of using data to learn something about the world. It’s a science since we’ll use well-developed tools to help us examine our data – you will learn ways to explore data (with graphics and numerical summaries) and to formally test whether the differences we see in our sample are big enough to convince us that they exist in the population (this is called statistical inference). It’s an art since there are human decisions everywhere, and no two statisticians will make exactly the same decisions – you’ll learn what to look for and develop an “eye” for the judgment calls that are necessary in statistics. The goal of statistics is to learn something true about the world using our data and to appropriately evaluate data-based claims that we find in the media or in our individual fields of study.

1.3.4 How to Thrive in Stat 201

Make (Regular) Time: You will be asked to think, compute, and write in this class. You will run into bugs in your code and practice the skill of troubleshooting. Start your homework early so that you have the opportunity to ask questions in office hours – and so that concepts have time to percolate!

Learn By Practicing: Research on how people learn shows that trying to remember or work through something is much better for your learning than just re-reading it – even if you get it wrong at first! Some ways you can incorporate this into your studying:

  • Work through examples on your own first: When you read the textbook, try to work the examples by yourself, then compare what you did to the book’s process and solution.
  • Test yourself: You can do this with flash cards for key concepts and definitions, practice problems, or even talking about what we did in class with classmates or friends (or a parent/grandparent – whoever will listen!). Fun fact: even unsuccessfully or incorrectly practicing recalling the material helps your brain strengthen the connections to it! Just make sure you look up the right answers later.
  • Try in-class activities, book problems, or other practice materials without referring to your notes first – then go back and check your work.

Read the Book: The textbook is a great resource, containing additional explanations of course concepts and an abundance of exercises. Re-read sections that were challenging for you and work through problems both on your own and with your classmates.

Ask Questions! You are always welcome (and encouraged!) to ask questions during class. Outside of class, you have a variety of options:

  • Attend TA sessions: TAs hold regular sessions where students work together on homework or course content and TAs are available to answer questions.
  • Come see me: You can come to my office hours, sign up for an appointment, or stop by and see if I’m available. If the door is open, you are welcome to drop in.
  • E-mail me: I reply to emails within 24 hours M-F (and usually sooner, and usually on weekends – but always within 24 hours M-F).
  • Get a tutor: As a Williams student, you can use the free tutoring services provided by the Peer Academic Support network. Step-by-step instructions for finding and scheduling tutoring sessions (drop-in or one-on-one) are on the Peer Academic Support webpage. If you have questions about tutoring at Williams, please email msrc@williams.edu.
